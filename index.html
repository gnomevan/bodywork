Touch Is a Conversation

This is what makes touch different.
You can see without being seen.
Hear without being heard.
But when hand meets skin, both sides feel it.
Contact is always mutual. Always a conversation.
This is what makes touch honest.
It cannot pretend separation.

Your Therapist Hears You. Your Body Needs the Same.

You know what happens when someone truly listens—not planning their response, just receiving what you’re saying. Something releases. The part holding the story alone finally softens.

Your body holds tension the way your mind holds stories.
Chronic bracing.
Shoulders that haven’t dropped in years.
Protective patterns that made sense once.

You can’t think your way out of this. You can understand the pattern, trace it back, name it—and still wake up clenched. Because the holding isn’t in your thoughts. It’s in your tissue, your nervous system, where words don’t reach.

In therapy, release comes from being heard. In the body, it comes from being felt.

Most Bodywork Misses This

Technique. Pressure. Muscles worked. Knots forced to release.
Sometimes that helps. But it isn’t the same as being met.
Being met means someone senses what your body is doing. Notices the bracing. Waits with the tension instead of pushing through it. Their attention says: I’m here. I feel this. You’re not alone.
That’s when the deepest holding releases.
Not because someone made it.
Because someone met it.

What Happens in a Session

We explore together—shoulders, neck, the space between your shoulder blades, your low back, hips, hamstrings, wrists. The places the body stores what it couldn’t process at the time.
I’m feeling for edges—places where protection begins. Not to push past them, but to meet them. To wait there until your nervous system decides it’s safe to soften.
Slowness. Movements subtle enough that your body can distinguish between strain and ease—and choose ease. Gravity doing the work. Rotation instead of force.
Two nervous systems in conversation—one speaking through hands, one through the softening or bracing of tissue. Both listening. Both responding.
Touch slow enough for your body to say no.
Attentive enough for your system to say yes.

Why I Do This

I’ve always been sensitive. It made me a good painter, a good sculptor—I can feel into materials, sense what wants to emerge.
But making art alone can be isolating. This work is different. When tension releases in someone else’s body, I feel it in mine too. The bilateral nature of touch means we’re both changed.
I’m fascinated by reciprocity—by reopening the feedback loops we’ve been trained to sever. Between our bodies and our attention. Between each other. Between us and the world we’re touching.
This is one way back to that conversation.
One nervous system meeting another and remembering:
we were never separate.

What People Say After

“I didn’t know I could feel that safe in my body.”
“I’ve been in therapy for years and never released that.”
“I didn’t realize how much I was holding.”
Or sometimes just:
“Oh.”
